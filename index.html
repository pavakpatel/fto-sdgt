Foreign Terrorist Organization
(FTO) Designation
and
Specially Designated Global Terrorist
(SDGT) Designation  

By Matthew Zweig

April XX, 2022

Taken together, the Foreign Terrorist Organization (FTO) and Specially Designated Global Terrorist (SDGT) designations pack a powerful one-two punch. Removing either would weaken Washington’s ability to target Iran’s Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC).

Impact
FTO
SDGT

Terror victims — including Gold Star Families — can sue for civil damages from sanctions violators

Yes

1
Over the past 30 years, Congress has repeatedly made it easier for American victims of terrorism to sue aiders and abettors of terrorism, by creating a private right of action against those that provide support to designated FTOs. In 2016, Congress expanded the ATA to provide an additional path for victims to sue — civil aiding-and-abetting and civil conspiracy liability — alongside the existing material-support threshold. In October 2018, Congress narrowed the “act of war” exception in the ATA’s civil liability provision by making it inapplicable to conflicts involving FTOs and SDGTs.

No

2
Executive Order 13224, the authority under which the U.S. government issues SDGT designations, contains no private right of action for victims of terrorism. The Justice for United States Victims of State Sponsors of Terrorism Act does allow for criminal penalties and civil fines to be used for the US Victims of State Sponsors of Terrorism fund, but it does not allow victims of terrorism to sue those that provide material support to FTOs.

Allows for U.S. government asset freezes/financial sanctions

Yes

3
(for entities in U.S. jurisdiction only)
Under the FTO designation, any individual or entity in the United States or otherwise subject to U.S. jurisdiction is prohibited from conducting transactions with the government of a designated state sponsor of terrorism. It is also unlawful for any such individual or entity to knowingly provide “material support or resources” to a designated FTO, and U.S. financial institutions may be required to block all FTO assets.

Yes

4
(for entities anywhere connected to U.S. financial system)
In the aftermath of the 9/11 terrorist attacks, the Bush administration issued Executive Order 13224 for SDGT designations, providing expanded legal authorities for the State Department and Treasury Department to target such individuals and entities. Specifically, the authority enabled the United States to target terrorist financiers that access the U.S. financial system. In 2019, the executive order was expanded to include the application of secondary sanctions on any individuals or entities, including businesses, that allow their services to be used by SDGTs, further increasing the risk associated with providing financial services to terrorists.

Bans U.S. visa issuance and admission into the United States

Yes

5
Representatives and members of a designated FTO, if they are aliens, are inadmissible to, and in certain circumstances removable from, the United States. There are multiple indices and criteria for immigration restrictions.

No

6
There are no immigration restrictions attached to the SDGT designation.

Low Legal threshold for criminal prosecution for material support to terrorist organizations

Yes

7
(only have to provide that violators should have known that they were providing support to a terrorist organization)
The prosecution must prove only that the defendant “knowingly” provided material support or resources to an FTO. Such a crime is punishable by life in prison if the material support or resources provided to an FTO resulted in the death of any person. That penalty would disappear if the IRGC were delisted.

No

8
(violators would have to act willfully to provide support to a terrorist organization)
Criminal prosecution for engaging in a significant transaction with the IRGC in violation of Executive Order 13224 requires the prosecution to prove that the defendant willfully violated sanctions against the FTO, but normally relies on strict liability standard.

Extraterritorial Application

Yes

9
(explicitly stated)
The extraterritorial application is explicitly stated throughout the statute with respect to material support to an FTO. For example, the FTO statute allows for prosecution in the United States for conspiring to provide material support or resources to, or for receiving military-type training from, a designated FTO, or for engaging in other terroristic activities, even when such conduct was committed entirely outside of the United States by non-U.S. nationals.

Yes

10
(not explicitly stated)
The criminal penalties associated with an SDGT designation apply to all individuals or entities subject to U.S. jurisdiction, wherever located, and to anyone that causes such an individual or entity to violate these terrorism sanctions deliberately or inadvertently.

Criminal prosecution for providing material support to terrorist organizations

Yes

11
(up to life, for anyone who “knowingly” supports — a lower standard to prove)
Severe criminal penalties are applied throughout the FTO statute, including with respect to providing material support to a terrorist organization. Specifically, anyone who knowingly provides material support or resources to an FTO or attempts or conspires to do so could face imprisonment of up to 20 years or, if it results the death of any person, life imprisonment.

Yes

12
(up to 20 years, for anyone who “willfully” supports — a higher standard to prove)
Under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), from which the criminal penalties for the SDGT designation are derived, any person that “willfully commits, willfully attempts to commit, or willfully conspires to commit, or aids or abets in the commission of” providing material support to an SDGT “shall, upon conviction, be fined not more than $1,000,000, or if a natural person, may be imprisoned for not more than 20 years, or both.” The knowledge standard — “willfully” — is much higher than that in the FTO provision. 

Civil Fines and Property Forfeitures

Yes

13
The FTO statute includes civil penalties for noncompliance, particularly with respect to financial institutions that fail to comply with freezing certain accounts. Each violation carries a civil penalty of $50,000 or twice the amount of which the financial institution was required to retain possession or control.

Yes

14
Under IEEPA, anyone who violates sanctions is subject to a civil penalty of $250,000 or twice the amount of the transaction that is the basis for the penalty. 

Removing the IRGC from the FTO list or SDGT list, or otherwise weakening the application of sanctions without a change in behavior sets a dangerous precedent and will undermine the integrity of terrorism sanctions.
